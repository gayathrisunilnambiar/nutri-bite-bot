\begin{abstract}
Patients diagnosed with hypertension~(HTN), Type~2 Diabetes
Mellitus~(T2DM), and Chronic Kidney Disease~(CKD), whether
independently or as co-existing conditions, face conflicting
dietary constraints across sodium, potassium, protein, and
carbohydrate intake. Existing clinical nutrition systems
do not automatically reconcile these conflicts at the ingredient or portion
level. To address these gaps, this paper proposes an interpretable end-to-end clinical decision support system
that generates personalised ingredient-level dietary portion
recommendations from 14~EHR (Electronic Health Records) - derived clinical features
across four stages. A computer vision module first automatically detects
available ingredients from a user-submitted image. Four
independently trained TabNet classifiers then infer
per-nutrient sensitivity levels~(Low/Moderate/High) for
sodium, potassium, protein, and carbohydrate, producing
continuous severity scores and native per-instance feature
attribution masks via sparsemax attention. A five-constraint
sigmoid-weighted portion computation engine subsequently
translates these scores into ingredient-specific maximum
safe gram quantities against a session-persistent daily
nutrient budget. The proposed system also incorporates a cosine-similarity
substitution engine for contra-indicated ingredients and a
guideline-compliant constraint relaxation mechanism that
prevents caloric collapse without violating potassium and
sodium safety limits. All components execute within a local
Docker environment to ensure compliance and data privacy. 
The TabNet clinical multi-target sensitivity classifier achieves 99.91\% mean
accuracy and 99.91\% weighted F1~score across four nutrient
sensitivity targets - sodium, potassium, protein, and
carbohydrate - with mean Cohen's~$\kappa = 0.9983$.
TabNet's suitability for clinical deployment stems from its
native per-instance interpretability: attention-based
feature attribution masks produced during inference directly
reveal the laboratory indicators driving each prediction,
enabling transparent and clinically explainable dietary
recommendations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
This paper presents NutriBiteBot, an interpretable clinical
decision support system, containerized for local deployment,
that generates personalised ingredient-level dietary portion
recommendations for patients with hypertension~(HTN), Type~2
Diabetes Mellitus~(T2DM), and Chronic Kidney Disease~(CKD),
whether presenting independently or as co-existing conditions. All
components execute within a local Docker environment, ensuring
patient data never reaches a public network.

These three conditions collectively impose some of the most
contradictory dietary requirements in clinical practice. The DASH
diet for HTN prescribes high potassium ; CKD
management mandates strict potassium restriction to prevent fatal
hyperkalaemia~\cite{kdigo2024}. Adequate dietary protein supports
glycaemic control in T2DM, while CKD requires protein restriction  to slow renal decline~\cite{kdoqi2020,ada2024}.
No existing system resolves these conflicts automatically, and no model
translates clinical nutrient limits into ingredient-level gram
quantities anchored to the patient's actual laboratory
values~\cite{diet_hrs_scoping2025,marashi2023}. Current practice
instead depends on manual dietitian consultation, a per patient process that is prone to error when three or more
conflicting guideline sets must be reconciled simultaneously, and
one constrained by a severe national shortage of registered
dietitians. Furthermore, most Indian
households rely on ingredients catalogued in the Indian Food
Composition Tables~(IFCT~2017)~\cite{ifct2017}, a database entirely
absent from international dietary applications.

NutriBiteBot addresses these gaps through an integrated architecture. A
multi-target TabNet classifier infers per-nutrient
sensitivity levels (Low/Moderate/High) for sodium, potassium,
protein, and carbohydrates from 14~EHR-derived clinical features, while producing native per-instance
feature attribution masks that enable patient-specific clinical
explanations at inference. A condition-adaptive dietary
management framework automatically resolves conflicting nutrient
constraints across independent or co-existing HTN, T2DM, and CKD,
enforcing the most restrictive clinically appropriate limit per
nutrient without manual intervention. A novel
five-constraint sigmoid severity-to-fraction portion computation
engine, grounded in IFCT~2017~\cite{ifct2017}, translates
continuous severity scores into ingredient-specific maximum safe
gram quantities against a live session-persistent daily nutrient
budget, with a cosine-similarity-based substitution engine for
contra-indicated ingredients and guideline-compliant caloric
sufficiency validation. All components execute within a
fully local Docker environment, ensuring patient
laboratory data never leaves the device infrastructure.
While designed and validated in the Indian clinical context, the
framework is generalisable to any region with an appropriate
national food composition database.

\section{Motivation and Contributions}
\label{sec:motivation}

The confluence of HTN, T2DM, and CKD in a single patient creates a
dietary management problem that is qualitatively distinct from managing
any one of these conditions in isolation. Each condition imposes its
own set of nutrient-level constraints derived from separate clinical
guideline bodies---AHA/ACC for hypertension, ADA for diabetes, and
KDIGO for chronic kidney disease---and these constraints are frequently
contradictory. The DASH diet prescribes high potassium intake
($\geq$4,700~mg/day) to lower blood pressure, while KDIGO mandates
potassium restriction ($\leq$2,000~mg/day at CKD Stage~3) to prevent
life-threatening hyperkalemia. Adequate dietary protein supports
glycaemic control in T2DM, yet CKD requires protein restriction
(0.6--0.8~g/kg/day) to retard uraemic toxin accumulation and slow
renal decline. No existing clinical nutrition tool resolves these
conflicts automatically, and none translates reconciled nutrient
budgets into ingredient-level gram quantities anchored to a patient's
actual laboratory values.

The practical consequences of this gap are severe in the Indian
context. Over 101~million Indians live with T2DM, approximately
220~million with HTN, and an estimated 17.2\% of the adult population
is affected by CKD~\cite{anjana2023}---conditions that co-occur at
high rates given their shared pathological mechanisms. Yet India faces
a critical shortage of registered dietitians, estimated at 1~per
333,000~population~\cite{varma2023}, with clinical
dietitian-to-patient ratios frequently exceeding 1:100 in accredited
hospitals and surpassing 1:200 in non-accredited
settings~\cite{phfi2012}. Each multi-guideline consultation requires
30--60~minutes of expert time and remains error-prone when three or
more conflicting constraint sets must be reconciled simultaneously.
Furthermore, international dietary applications such as MyFitnessPal,
Cronometer, and Nutritics are built on Western food composition
databases and do not incorporate the Indian Food Composition Tables
(IFCT~2017)~\cite{ifct2017}, rendering them inapplicable to the
staple ingredients consumed by Indian patients. These gaps collectively
define the clinical and operational need that NutriBiteBot is designed
to address.

A secondary motivation is interpretability. Clinical deployment of
machine learning models requires that predictions be explainable to
clinicians and patients. Post-hoc explanation
methods such as SHAP introduce additional computational overhead and
are decoupled from the model's inference mechanism, raising the risk
of explanation infidelity~\cite{arik2021tabnet}. A model that produces
per-instance feature attributions natively during the forward pass is
structurally preferable for clinical use, as attributions can be
directly linked to patient laboratory values and governing guideline
references at the point of recommendation.

The core contributions of this work are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A multi-target TabNet classifier that infers per-nutrient
    sensitivity levels (Low/Moderate/High) for sodium, potassium,
    protein, and carbohydrates from 14~EHR-derived clinical
    features---namely age; sex\_male; bmi (body mass index); sbp and
    dbp (systolic and diastolic blood pressure); has\_htn, has\_dm, and
    has\_ckd (binary indicators for hypertension, diabetes, and chronic
    kidney disease); creatinine (kidney function marker); egfr
    (estimated kidney filtration rate); serum\_sodium and
    serum\_potassium (electrolyte levels); and hba1c and fbs (long-term
    and fasting blood glucose measures)---achieving mean accuracy
    99.91\% and mean weighted F1~99.91\% with mean Cohen's
    $\kappa = 0.9983$.

    \item A condition adaptive dietary management framework that
    evaluates both independent and co-existing HTN, T2DM, and CKD,
    automatically resolving conflicting nutrient constraints and
    enforcing clinically appropriate limits.

    \item A novel five-constraint sigmoid severity-to-fraction portion
    computation formulation that produces ingredient-specific maximum
    safe gram quantities from continuous severity scores against a live
    daily nutrient budget.
    
    \item A fully local Docker deployment that ensures patient
    laboratory data never reaches a public network, satisfying
    DPDPA~2023 requirements.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Survey}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Clinical Dietary Decision Support Systems}
\label{subsec:rw_cdss}

Existing clinical nutrition tools fall into two broad categories:
consumer macronutrient trackers and professional dietitian platforms.
Consumer tools (MyFitnessPal, Cronometer, Healthify~Me) perform
calorie accounting with no disease-specific constraint logic and no
mechanism for resolving inter-condition dietary conflicts. Professional
platforms (Nutritics, Computrition) provide nutrient analysis but
require a registered dietitian to manually configure every constraint
and resolve conflicts across co-existing conditions, precisely the
bottleneck that NutriBiteBot automates. A recent scoping review of
fifteen diet-related health recommender systems found that while 86\%
employ hybrid recommendation approaches, none automatically resolve
conflicting nutrient constraints arising from co-existing
conditions~\cite{diet_hrs_scoping2025}. A systematic review of AI
applications for nutritional care in CKD also found that, of 2,053
screened studies, only seven met criteria for personalised dietary
recommendation using patient-specific clinical data, and none resolved
multi-guideline conflicts at the ingredient
level~\cite{ai_ckd_diet2025}. A data-driven nutrient recommendation framework for personalised meal planning proposed by Kumar~\textit{et al.}~\cite{kumar2025} similarly demonstrated the feasibility of ML-driven dietary personalisation but did not address multi-condition conflict
resolution or ingredient-level portion grounding. Food recommendation
for CKD patients using collaborative filtering has also been
explored~\cite{banerjee2019}, though without grounding in
patient-specific laboratory values or integration of Indian food
composition data.

The closest prior work to NutriBiteBot in the multi-comorbidity
dietary CDSS space is the fuzzy logic system of
Marashi-Hosseini~\textit{et al.}~\cite{marashi2023}, which applies
Mamdani inference with 1,144~rules to adjust diets for patients with
HTN, T2DM, hyperlipidaemia, and kidney disease simultaneously,
reporting 97\% agreement with dietitian-set diets on 100~clinical
records. While demonstrating that automated multi-condition dietary
reconciliation is feasible, this system requires manual rule authoring
by a dietitian, operates at the macronutrient level without
ingredient-level gram quantities, and has no mechanism for grounding
constraints in patient-specific laboratory values such as eGFR or
serum potassium.

In contrast, the closest widely deployed solution in the Indian
context, HealthifyMe, routes user data through cloud-based
infrastructure and functions predominantly as a calorie-tracking
platform. It does not enforce strict gram-level clinical constraints
or support deterministic dietary ceilings required for disease
management, raising compliance concerns under the Digital Personal
Data Protection Act~2023~\cite{chandrasekaran2025dpdpa}.

\subsection{Machine Learning for Clinical Tabular Data}
\label{subsec:rw_ml}

Learning from tabular EHR data with heterogeneous feature types and
modest sample sizes remains an active research challenge. Gradient
boosting methods---XGBoost~\cite{chen2016xgboost},
LightGBM, and NGBoost~\cite{duan2020ngboost}---dominate tabular
benchmarks due to their inductive bias for heterogeneous feature
distributions and resistance to overfitting. NGBoost extends gradient
boosting to probabilistic output via natural gradient estimation,
producing per-prediction uncertainty estimates that carry direct
clinical interpretability value. These methods have been applied to
EHR-derived comorbidity prediction in the HTN--T2DM space,
demonstrating strong performance on primary care
datasets~\cite{gollapalli2022}.

TabNet~\cite{arik2021tabnet} introduced an attention-based deep
architecture for tabular data that learns sparse feature selection
masks at each sequential decision step, producing native per-instance
feature attribution without requiring post-hoc methods such as SHAP.
Clinical deployments of TabNet have demonstrated its suitability for
EHR-based risk prediction tasks:
Yasmeen~\textit{et al.}~\cite{yasmeen2025tabnet} showed that a hybrid
TabNet--XGBoost stacked ensemble achieved strong performance on
cardiovascular risk prediction from heterogeneous patient records, with
the TabNet component providing clinically interpretable sparse feature
selection. Similarly, Cao~\textit{et al.}~\cite{cao2025tabnet} applied
TabNet to predicting chemotherapy-induced cardiotoxicity from routine
laboratory and clinical data, emphasising interpretability as a deployment requirement~\cite{bharati2024}. Recent work has further extended TabNet with graph-based dependency modelling for multi-disease coexistence risk prediction~\cite{gat_tabnet2025}. TabNet has also been applied to ICU admission prediction from clinical tabular data~\cite{houfani2022} and cardiovascular disease risk assessment in diabetic patients~\cite{zhang2024}, both confirming its suitability for high-stakes clinical classification from heterogeneous EHR features. Deep learning approaches for identifying prognostic predictors in CKD have further demonstrated the value of interpretable architectures in renal clinical settings~\cite{liang2023}.

\subsection{Monotonicity Constraints in Clinical Predictive Models}
\label{subsec:rw_monotone}

Physiologically motivated monotonicity constraints prevent clinical
models from learning counter-intuitive decision boundaries---for
example, predicting lower dietary restriction requirements at higher
serum creatinine. Isotonic regression, monotone neural networks, and
constrained gradient boosting have all been applied to enforce such
constraints in clinical prediction
settings~\cite{gupta2019}. Monotonic calibration via isotonic
regression is particularly relevant in the EHR setting where known
clinical directionality (declining eGFR $\Rightarrow$ worsening renal
function) should be preserved irrespective of distributional artefacts
in the training data. The combination of per-target monotonic
preprocessing with a native attention-based feature selector---as
employed in NutriBiteBot---has not previously been reported in the
clinical dietary recommendation literature.

\subsection{Computer Vision for Ingredient Detection}
\label{subsec:rw_cv}

Automated food and ingredient recognition from images has been
extensively studied as a component of dietary assessment and chronic
disease monitoring. A systematic review by
Mansouri~\textit{et al.}~\cite{mansouri2023food} covering deep
learning food recognition methods found that while YOLO-family and
CNN-based architectures achieve strong detection performance on
curated food datasets, recognition accuracy degrades for culturally
specific ingredients, processed foods, and items not well-represented
in Western-centric training corpora. This limitation is directly
relevant to the Indian kitchen ingredient domain targeted by
NutriBiteBot: IFCT-mapped staple ingredients (moong dal, toor dal,
bitter gourd, ridge gourd) exhibit substantially higher intra-class
visual variance than the standardised food items in international
benchmarks. The Roboflow-based detector used in NutriBiteBot's
Stage~1 pipeline addresses this through transfer learning on a curated
set of raw Indian ingredients, though coverage of packaged and
processed foods remains a documented limitation
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Large Language Models for Clinical Dietary Guidance}
\label{subsec:rw_llm}

Large language models have attracted growing interest as dietary guidance tools. However, benchmarking studies have consistently identified safety-critical failure modes in unconstrained LLM deployment for clinical nutrition. Kairat~\textit{et al.}~\cite{kairat2025llm} benchmarked multiple LLMs—including Llama-family models—for stage-specific CKD dietary recommendations and found that all off-the-shelf models produced clinically unsafe potassium and protein quantities in the absence of external constraint enforcement. Whittle~\textit{et al.}~\cite{whittle2025llm} reached a similar conclusion in their review of LLM-based dietary tools for CKD, explicitly recommending the integration of clinical guidelines as hard boundaries rather than soft prompting. These findings motivate the bounded LLM architecture of NutriBiteBot, in which Groq Llama-3.3 generates adapted recipes strictly within hard gram-level constraints pre-computed by the upstream rules engine—constraints that the agent cannot override. A recent system, NutriGen~\cite{khamesian2025}, explored LLM-based personalised meal plan generation and likewise concluded that, without hard clinical constraint enforcement, LLM outputs cannot be considered safe for patients with active chronic conditions. This further reinforces the architectural decision in NutriBiteBot to use the LLM solely as a bounded natural language renderer operating downstream of a deterministic rules engine.

\subsection{Data Privacy in Clinical AI Deployments in India}
\label{subsec:rw_privacy}

India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act~(DPDPA)~2023 imposes
obligations on any system collecting or processing patient health
data, including requirements for explicit consent, restrictions on
cross-border data transfers, and significant financial penalties for
data breaches~\cite{chandrasekaran2025dpdpa}. These requirements have direct architectural implications for clinical AI systems: any framework that transmits patient laboratory values to external APIs or cloud-hosted model endpoints is structurally non-compliant regardless of encryption measures applied in transit. Clinical AI systems
that route patient laboratory values through public cloud APIs---as
do all existing commercial dietary tools---are structurally
non-compliant with these requirements. NutriBiteBot's fully local
Docker deployment, in which no patient data reaches a public
network, is designed specifically to satisfy DPDPA~2023 data
sovereignty requirements and to support hospital on-premises and
clinic workstation installation.

\subsection{Research Gaps and Proposed Approach}
\label{subsec:gaps}

The literature survey above reveals five compounding gaps that no
existing system addresses simultaneously.

\textbf{No automatic multi-guideline conflict resolution at
the ingredient level.} Existing dietary CDSSs either operate on a
single condition~\cite{diet_hrs_scoping2025} or require manual rule
authoring by a registered dietitian~\cite{marashi2023}. None resolve
the conflicting constraints of KDIGO~2024~\cite{kdigo2024},
ADA~2024~\cite{ada2024}, and AHA/ACC~\cite{aha2017}
simultaneously and automatically for a patient presenting with all
three conditions, and none translate the resolution into
ingredient-level gram quantities.

\textbf{No patient-laboratory-grounded portion computation.}
Population-level nutrient thresholds (e.g., ``restrict potassium to
$\leq$2,000~mg/day'') are applied uniformly across existing systems.
No tool derives per-patient portion limits as a continuous function
of the patient's actual laboratory severity, such that a patient with
eGFR~$=$~42~mL/min and one with eGFR~$=$~28~mL/min receive
proportionally different portion ceilings for the same ingredient.

\textbf{Absence of Indian food composition data.}
International dietary tools are grounded in USDA or European food
composition databases. IFCT~2017~\cite{ifct2017}---the authoritative
Indian composition database---is absent from all reviewed systems,
making their nutrient computations inapplicable to the staple
ingredients of Indian households.

\textbf{Unsafe unconstrained LLM outputs in clinical
nutrition.} Benchmarking studies demonstrate that unconstrained LLMs
produce clinically unsafe dietary quantities in CKD and
multi-comorbidity settings~\cite{kairat2025llm,whittle2025llm}. No
prior system has integrated an LLM within a bounded architecture that
enforces hard gram-level clinical safety constraints computed upstream
by a rules engine.

\textbf{Cloud dependency and DPDPA non-compliance.} All
deployed consumer and professional dietary tools route patient data
through cloud infrastructure~\cite{chandrasekaran2025dpdpa}, making
them structurally non-compliant with DPDPA~2023 and unsuitable for
deployment in Indian hospital settings where data sovereignty
requirements are binding.

\textbf{Proposed approach.} NutriBiteBot addresses all five gaps
through an integrated eight-layer architecture: (i)~a multi-target
TabNet classifier trained on MIMIC-IV EHR data infers per-nutrient
sensitivity levels from 14~clinical variables, with per-target
monotonic preprocessing enforcing physiologically grounded
constraints; (ii)~a five-constraint sigmoid severity-to-fraction
portion computation engine derives ingredient-specific gram limits as
a continuous function of the patient's laboratory severity scores
against a live daily nutrient budget grounded in IFCT~2017; (iii)~a
KDIGO-2024-compliant hierarchical constraint relaxation hierarchy
prevents caloric collapse without violating potassium and sodium
safety limits; and (iv)~Groq Llama-3.3 operates as a bounded
generative agent, producing adapted recipes exclusively within the
hard gram-level boundaries enforced by the upstream rules engine. The
entire system executes within a local Docker environment, ensuring
full DPDPA~2023 compliance.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

NutriBiteBot is deployed as a locally containerised system comprising
five distinct architectural components: a single-page web frontend, a
Python/Flask backend server, a four-stage deep learning pipeline, a local
Supabase (PostgreSQL) database, and a Docker deployment environment
(Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}). All components execute within a local
Docker environment; patient laboratory data, generated recipes, and
clinical records never leave the device infrastructure. This
design satisfies the sensitivity requirements of India's DPDPA~2023.

\subsection{Frontend}

The user interface is a single-page web application (HTML5/CSS3/
JavaScript) exposing three functional panels: a Fridge Scanner
(refrigerator image upload and ingredient display), a Patient Data
Input form (14~clinical features), and a Recipe Generator panel (safe
portion recommendations and adapted recipe). The frontend communicates
exclusively via REST API calls to the backend and holds no patient
data in client-side storage.

\subsection{Backend Server}

The backend is a Python/Flask server that orchestrates DL inference
across the four-stage pipeline, aggregates rules engine constraint
outputs, and manages write operations to the local database. Backend
deep learning inference uses PyTorch as the TabNet runtime. Agent orchestration
logic sequences pipeline stages and manages error handling, fallback
logic, and caloric sufficiency validation.

\subsection{Tabnet Implementation Pipeline}

The four-stage pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:dlpipeline}) executes
sequentially: Vision Detection (Stage~1), TabNet Risk Stratification
(Stage~2), Clinical Rules Engine with Portion Computation (Stage~3),
and Bounded Generative Agent (Stage~4).

\subsection{Database}

Patient records and generated recipes are persisted to a Supabase
instance running on local PostgreSQL within the same Docker
environment. No data is written to Supabase's cloud infrastructure.
Stored entities include patient identifiers, clinical feature vectors,
per-session portion decision records, generated recipe outputs, and
daily nutrient ledger state.

\subsection{Constraints}

The following constraints are assumed to define the operational boundary of the proposed system. The Roboflow ingredient detection model is assumed to operate on raw ingredients commonly found in Indian kitchens; packaged foods, processed items, and uncommon produce are therefore considered outside the assumed input domain. The dietary rule engine is assumed to address four clinical conditions: hypertension (HTN), type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM), chronic kidney disease (CKD), and optional dyslipidaemia. Each disease is supported by well-defined dietary rules and validated sensitivity classifiers; extension to additional comorbidities is assumed to fall outside the scope of the current framework.

\subsection{Deployment}

The complete system is containerised via a single
\texttt{docker-compose} configuration that defines all inter-service
networking. No component is exposed to the public internet by
default. This model supports hospital on-premises installation,
clinic workstations, and telemedicine platforms operating within a
secured local network.

\section{Design and Implementation}
\label{sec:dlpipeline}

\subsection{Dataset Specification}
\label{subsec:dataset}

Training data are drawn from MIMIC-IV
(version~2.2)~\cite{mimic4,mimic4}, a publicly available
de-identified EHR database containing patient measurements,
laboratory tests, diagnoses, treatments, and clinical notes. For
each patient encounter, a 14-dimensional clinical feature vector is
extracted comprising age, sex, BMI, systolic and diastolic blood
pressure~(SBP, DBP), serum sodium, serum potassium, creatinine,
estimated glomerular filtration rate~(eGFR), HbA1c, fasting blood
sugar~(FBS), and binary flags for hypertension~(HTN), diabetes
mellitus~(DM), and chronic kidney disease~(CKD). The dataset is
partitioned into training and held-out test sets using an 80/20
stratified split on \texttt{sodium\_sensitivity} to preserve class
balance across all targets.

A threshold-based \texttt{LabelGenerator} assigns training labels
using clinically grounded rules derived from established medical
guidelines, applied \emph{offline during dataset construction only}
and not used during inference. Each target is a three-class ordinal
variable: Low~(0), Moderate~(1), or High~(2). Four nutrient
sensitivity targets are generated: \textbf{sodium\_sensitivity}
(serum Na, HTN flag, SBP; AHA/ACC guidelines);
\textbf{potassium\_sensitivity} (serum K, eGFR; KDIGO~2024);
\textbf{protein\_restriction} (eGFR, creatinine, CKD flag;
KDIGO~2024); and \textbf{carb\_sensitivity} (HbA1c, FBS, BMI, DM
flag; ADA~2024). Phosphorus sensitivity is derived deterministically
from eGFR and CKD status via KDIGO-based thresholds and is not
treated as a learned target. The labelling thresholds are summarised
in Table~\ref{tab:label_rules}.

\subsection{Data Integrity and Spurious Correlation Mitigation}
\label{subsec:spurious}

During early validation, \texttt{carb\_sensitivity} incorrectly
assigned high risk to patients with severe renal impairment but
normal glycaemic markers, indicating spurious correlations between
renal features and carbohydrate labels. Three contamination channels
were identified: (i)~synthetic sampling occasionally produced
borderline HbA1c values for non-diabetic patients; (ii)~a shared
monotonic preprocessor propagated renal signals into the
carbohydrate model; and (iii)~BMI co-occurrence patterns in the
CKD+HTN subgroup triggered carbohydrate sensitivity labels despite
normal glycaemia.

Mitigations comprised constraining glycaemic distributions for
non-diabetic patients, training independent per-target
\texttt{MonotonicFeatureTransformer} instances, and tightening BMI
distributions for CKD+HTN patients without diabetes.
Post-mitigation validation confirmed that the Pearson correlation
between HbA1c and eGFR in the CKD+no-DM subgroup satisfies
$|r| < 0.15$ prior to label generation, and that renal features
(eGFR, creatinine, \texttt{has\_ckd}) are absent from the top-5
TabNet attribution for \texttt{carb\_sensitivity} on any patient
with normal glycaemic markers, confirming that predictions depend
exclusively on glycaemic features.


\subsection{Preprocessing and Monotonic Constraints}
\label{subsec:preprocessing}

Raw features undergo median imputation for missing laboratory values
followed by $z$-score scaling. Each sensitivity target then receives
its own \texttt{MonotonicFeatureTransformer}, which applies isotonic
regression to enforce physiologically grounded directional
relationships prior to model input. Each target $k$ receives an
independently fitted transformer $\mathcal{T}_k$ trained exclusively
on $y_k$:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{T}_k = \underset{\mathcal{T}}{\arg\min}\;
  \sum_{i=1}^{N}
  \bigl(\mathcal{T}(x_{i,j}) - y_{i,k}\bigr)^2
  \quad \forall\, j \in \mathcal{C}
  \label{eq:isotonic_exp}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathcal{C}$ is the set of constrained feature indices. This
eliminates the cross-target contamination identified in
Section~\ref{subsec:spurious}, where a shared transformer encoded
renal signals into the carbohydrate sensitivity model's transformed
feature space.

Positive monotonicity constraints~($+1$) are assigned to creatinine
and serum~K (worsening renal function per
KDIGO~2024~\cite{kdigo2024}); HbA1c and FBS (glycaemic control per
ADA~2024~\cite{ada2024}); SBP (sodium restriction stringency per
AHA/ACC~\cite{aha2017}); and DBP, BMI, and serum~Na. A negative
constraint~($-1$) is applied to eGFR, since declining filtration
requires progressively tighter potassium, phosphorus, and protein
restriction per KDIGO~2024~\cite{kdigo2024}.

Formally, for any two patients $a$ and $b$, a positive constraint
on feature $j$ enforces:
%
\begin{equation}
  x_{a,j} \leq x_{b,j}
  \implies
  \hat{y}_k(\mathbf{x}_a) \leq \hat{y}_k(\mathbf{x}_b)
  \label{eq:monotone_pos}
\end{equation}
%
and the negative constraint on eGFR enforces:
%
\begin{equation}
  x_{a,\,\text{eGFR}} \leq x_{b,\,\text{eGFR}}
  \implies
  \hat{y}_k(\mathbf{x}_a) \geq \hat{y}_k(\mathbf{x}_b)
  \label{eq:monotone_neg}
\end{equation}
%
These constraints prevent counter-physiological decision boundaries
irrespective of distributional artefacts in the training data.

\subsection{TabNet Architecture and Training}
\label{subsec:tabnet}

TabNet~\cite{arik2021tabnet} processes tabular features through
$N_{\text{steps}}$ sequential decision steps, each producing a sparse
attention mask over the $F=14$ input features via a sparsemax
activation:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}^{(b)}_i =
  \text{sparsemax}\!\left(
    \mathbf{P}^{(b-1)}_i \odot
    \mathbf{W}_{\text{att}} \cdot
    \mathbf{a}^{(b-1)}_i
  \right)
  \label{eq:sparsemax}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{P}^{(b-1)}_i$ penalises features selected in prior
steps, $\mathbf{a}^{(b-1)}_i$ is the preceding step's intermediate
representation, and $\mathbf{W}_{\text{att}}$ is a learnable weight
matrix. The sparsemax activation assigns exactly zero weight to
irrelevant features, enforcing clinically legible sparse selection.
Per-step masks are averaged to yield a per-patient feature attribution
mask:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{m}_i =
  \frac{1}{N_{\text{steps}}}
  \sum_{b=1}^{N_{\text{steps}}}
  \mathbf{h}^{(b)}_i
  \label{eq:attribution}
\end{equation}

Because $\mathbf{m}_i$ is a by-product of the forward pass, TabNet
provides native per-instance interpretability without post-hoc methods
such as SHAP.

Four independent \texttt{TabNetClassifier} models are trained, one
per sensitivity target. Each outputs a class-probability vector
$\mathbf{p}_i = [P(\text{Low}),\, P(\text{Moderate}),\,
P(\text{High})]$, from which a continuous severity score is derived:
\begin{equation}
  s_i =
  0 \cdot P(\text{Low})
  + 1 \cdot P(\text{Moderate})
  + 2 \cdot P(\text{High}),
  \quad s_i \in [0,\,2]
  \label{eq:severity}
\end{equation}

$s_i$ replaces the discrete class label as input to the portion
computation engine (Section~\ref{sec:portion}), preserving
sub-threshold severity gradients when computing dietary constraints.
Training uses early stopping (patience~$=15$, maximum 100~epochs)
with GPU acceleration; inference runs on CPU to match the Docker
deployment configuration.

\section{Portion Computation and Caloric Validation}
\label{sec:portion}

The portion computation engine translates continuous severity scores
from the TabNet classifier into ingredient-specific maximum safe gram
quantities. A caloric sufficiency validator then verifies nutritional
adequacy and applies guideline-compliant constraint relaxation if required.

\subsection{Sigmoid Severity-to-Fraction Function}
\label{subsec:sigmoid}

For each IFCT-mapped ingredient, a sigmoid severity-to-fraction
function converts each nutrient's continuous severity score
$s \in [0,\,2]$ to a budget utilisation fraction $f(s)$:
%
\begin{equation}
  f(s) = 0.08 +
  \frac{0.32}{1 + \exp\!\bigl(3(s - 1.0)\bigr)}
  \label{eq:sigmoid}
\end{equation}
%
This produces $f(0.0) = 0.40$, $f(1.0) = 0.24$, and
$f(2.0) = 0.08$ with a smooth transition through Moderate
(Fig.~\ref{fig:sigmoid}). The 0.08 lower bound ensures a non-zero
allocation even at maximum severity.

\begin{figure}[!t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{sigmoid_severity}
    \caption{Sigmoid severity-to-fraction function $f(s)$
    (Eq.~\eqref{eq:sigmoid}).
    $f(0.0)=0.40$ (Low);
    $f(1.0)=0.24$ (Moderate);
    $f(2.0)=0.08$ (High).}
    \label{fig:sigmoid}
\end{figure}

% -------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Per-Constraint Gram Computation}
\label{subsec:grams}

For each nutrient $n \in \{\text{Na, K, protein, carbs,
phosphorus}\}$, the maximum allowable grams per ingredient are:
%
\begin{equation}
  g_n = \frac{R_n \times f(s_n)}{c_n / 100}
  \label{eq:grams}
\end{equation}
%
where $R_n$ is the remaining daily budget for nutrient $n$, $s_n$
is the severity score, and $c_n$ is the IFCT content per 100~g.
The binding maximum portion is:
%
\begin{equation}
  g^* = \min\!\bigl(\min_n(g_n),\; 300\bigr) \text{ g}
  \label{eq:binding}
\end{equation}
%
The 300~g cap prevents physiologically implausible portions.

% -------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Phosphorus--Protein Efficiency Adjustment}
\label{subsec:phos}

For CKD patients with $s_{\text{phos}} > 1.5$, an efficiency
adjustment is applied based on the phosphorus-to-protein ratio:
%
\begin{equation}
  \rho^{(i)} =
  \frac{c_{\text{phos}}^{(i)}}
       {\max\!\bigl(c_{\text{prot}}^{(i)},\; 0.1\bigr)}
  \label{eq:phos_ratio}
\end{equation}
%
Ingredients with $\rho^{(i)} < 15$~mg/g receive a 20\% portion
bonus; those with $\rho^{(i)} \geq 20$~mg/g receive a 25\%
penalty, consistent with KDIGO 2024~\cite{kdigo2024}.

% -------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Portion Decision Classification}
\label{subsec:label}

Each ingredient is assigned a label based on $g^*$:
%
\begin{equation}
  \text{label} =
  \begin{cases}
    \textsc{Avoid}        & g^* \leq 5\,\text{g} \\
    \textsc{Half Portion} & 5 < g^* \leq 75\,\text{g} \\
    \textsc{Allowed}      & g^* > 75\,\text{g}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:label}
\end{equation}

The complete five-stage procedure is formalised in Algorithm~\ref{alg:portion}.

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{Ingredient-Level Portion Computation}
\label{alg:portion}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require
    Ingredient list $\mathcal{I}$;
    severity scores $\{s_n\}$;
    daily budget $\{R_n\}$;
    IFCT database; CKD status flag
\Ensure
    Portion decision for each ingredient:
    grams allowed $g^*$, label, binding constraint
\Statex
\For{each ingredient $i \in \mathcal{I}$}
    \State \textbf{Step 1:} Fetch nutrient content $c_n^{(i)}$ from IFCT
    \Statex
    \State \textbf{Step 2:} For each nutrient $n$:
    \State \quad Compute budget fraction:
    \[
    f(s_n) = 0.08 + \frac{0.32}{1+\exp(3(s_n-1))}
    \]
    \State \quad Compute gram limit: $g_n^{(i)} = R_n \cdot f(s_n) / (c_n^{(i)}/100)$
    \Statex
    \State \textbf{Step 3:} If CKD and $s_{\text{phos}} > 1.5$:
    \State \quad Compute ratio: $\rho^{(i)} = c_{\text{phos}}^{(i)} / \max(c_{\text{prot}}^{(i)},\; 0.1)$
    \State \quad If $\rho^{(i)} < 15$: multiply $g_{\text{phos}}^{(i)}$ by $1.20$
    \State \quad If $\rho^{(i)} \geq 20$: multiply $g_{\text{phos}}^{(i)}$ by $0.75$
    \Statex
    \State \textbf{Step 4:} Find binding constraint:
    \[
    g^{*(i)} = \min\!\left(\min_n\, g_n^{(i)},\; 300\right)
    \]
    \Statex
    \State \textbf{Step 5:} Assign label:
    \If{$g^{*(i)} \leq 5$}
        \State label $\gets$ \textsc{Avoid}
    \ElsIf{$g^{*(i)} \leq 75$}
        \State label $\gets$ \textsc{Half Portion}
    \Else
        \State label $\gets$ \textsc{Allowed}
    \EndIf
    \Statex
    \State \textbf{Output:} (ingredient, $g^{*(i)}$, label, binding nutrient)
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

% -------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Substitution Engine}
\label{subsec:substitution}

For ingredients labelled \textsc{Avoid}, a cosine-similarity
substitution engine identifies up to three safe alternatives from
the same IFCT food category. Candidates are re-ranked by:
%
\begin{equation}
  \text{score}^{(j)} =
  0.60 \times
  \cos\!\bigl(\mathbf{v}^{(i)}, \mathbf{v}^{(j)}\bigr)
  + \frac{0.40}{c_{b}^{(j)}}
  \label{eq:substitution}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathbf{v}$ is the normalised nutrient feature vector
$[\text{protein, carbs, fat, fibre, calories}]$ per 100~g and
$c_b^{(j)}$ is the binding-constraint nutrient density of
candidate $j$. Each candidate is passed through
Algorithm~\ref{alg:portion} and accepted only if it does not
itself receive \textsc{Avoid}.

% -------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Caloric Sufficiency Validation}
\label{subsec:caloric}

After Algorithm~\ref{alg:portion} produces a full recommendation
set, projected session caloric intake is computed. If it falls
below 1{,}200~kcal, a guideline-compliant relaxation hierarchy
is applied (Algorithm~\ref{alg:caloric}). Potassium and sodium
are permanently immutable: hyperkalaemia and acute BP elevation
carry immediate mortality risk and cannot be traded for caloric
adequacy. Phosphorus, protein, and carbohydrate restrictions are
relaxed in that order, as each tolerates a single-session
adjustment without acute clinical consequence.
\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{Caloric Sufficiency Validation and Constraint Relaxation}
\label{alg:caloric}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require
    Portion recommendations $\mathcal{D}$ from Algorithm~\ref{alg:portion};
    severity scores $\{s_n\}$ for all nutrients;
    IFCT database with caloric content and nutrient density;
    minimum caloric threshold: 1200~kcal
\Statex
\State \textbf{Step 1: Compute total projected caloric intake}
\[
E = \sum_{i:\,\text{label} \neq \textsc{Avoid}} c_{\text{kcal}}^{(i)} \times g^{*(i)} / 100
\]
\If{$E \geq 1200$~kcal}
    \State \Return $\mathcal{D}$ (adequate), empty log
\EndIf
\Statex
\State \textbf{Step 2: Apply constraint relaxation hierarchy}
\State \textbf{Safety invariant:} Do not modify $s_{\text{K}}$ or $s_{\text{Na}}$ (immediate mortality risk)
\Statex
\If{$s_{\text{phos}} > 0$ \textbf{and} $E < 1200$~kcal}
    \State Relax: $s_{\text{phos}} \gets \max(0.90 \times s_{\text{phos}},\; 0)$
    \State Recompute $\mathcal{D}$ and update $E$
\EndIf
\If{$E < 1200$~kcal}
    \State Relax: $s_{\text{prot}} \gets \max(s_{\text{prot}} - 0.30,\; 0)$
    \State Recompute $\mathcal{D}$ and update $E$
\EndIf
\If{$E < 1200$~kcal}
    \State Relax: $s_{\text{carb}} \gets \max(s_{\text{carb}} - 0.30,\; 0)$
    \State Recompute $\mathcal{D}$ and update $E$
\EndIf
\Statex
\State \textbf{Step 3: Verify safety invariants}
\State Assert $s_{\text{K}}$ and $s_{\text{Na}}$ remain unchanged throughout
\Statex
\State \textbf{Output:} Updated recommendations $\mathcal{D}$; relaxation log with applied changes
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

across all sensitivity targets. Four independent models are trained

metrics account for class imbalance across the Low, Moderate, and

\section{Clinical Use Case Validation}
\label{sec:usecase}

This section demonstrates end-to-end system behaviour on a
representative patient presenting with co-existing CKD, HTN, and
T2DM---the primary target comorbidity profile of NutriBiteBot.
Outputs are validated against KDIGO~2024~\cite{kdigo2024},
ADA~2024~\cite{ada2024}, and AHA/ACC~\cite{aha2017} clinical
guidelines.

\subsection{Patient Profile and Risk Stratification}
\label{subsec:usecase_risk}

A 58-year-old male presents with CKD Stage~3
(eGFR~$=$~45~mL/min/1.73m$^2$) and uncontrolled hypertension
(SBP~$=$~162~mmHg, DBP~$=$~96~mmHg), without diabetes mellitus.
His laboratory values include serum potassium of 5.1~mmol/L,
creatinine of 1.9~mg/dL, serum sodium of 140~mEq/L,
HbA1c of 5.2\%, fasting blood sugar of 85~mg/dL, and
BMI of 26~kg/m$^2$. His 14-feature clinical vector is
input to the system. TabNet produces the following
sensitivity outputs at 100\% model confidence across all
four targets, each paired with the dominant attribution
feature and governing guideline:

\begin{enumerate}
\sloppy

    \item \textbf{Sodium HIGH} (severity~$\approx$~2.0)---driven by
    SBP~$=$~162~mmHg and HTN flag;
    AHA/ACC sodium limit $\leq$\,1{,}500~mg/day applies.

    \item \textbf{Potassium HIGH} (severity~$\approx$~2.0)---driven by
    eGFR~$=$~45~mL/min and serum K~$=$~5.1~mmol/L;
    KDIGO~2024 potassium ceiling $\leq$\,2{,}000~mg/day applies.

    \item \textbf{Protein HIGH} (severity~$\approx$~2.0)---driven by
    eGFR~$=$~45~mL/min and creatinine~$=$~1.9~mg/dL;
    KDIGO~2024 protein ceiling 0.6--0.8~g/kg/day applies.

    \item \textbf{Carbohydrate HIGH} (severity~$\approx$~2.0)---driven by
    HbA1c~$=$~5.2\% and BMI~$=$~26 in the presence of CKD;
    ADA~2024 carbohydrate restriction applies.

\end{enumerate}

The resulting daily nutrient budget is: Na~$=$~1{,}500~mg,
K~$=$~2{,}000~mg, protein~$=$~42~g, carbohydrates~$=$~275~g,
phosphorus~$=$~800~mg. All four nutrient sensitivity targets
are classified HIGH at 100\% model confidence. Despite
uniform HIGH sensitivity, the portion computation engine
produces three distinct binding constraints across the eight
detected ingredients, as the per-nutrient severity scores
interact with the IFCT-mapped nutrient densities of each
ingredient to determine which budget is exhausted first.

proportionally more of the carbohydrate budget before
potassium becomes binding. All eight \texttt{PortionDecision}
outputs were verified manually against KDIGO~2024 and
AHA/ACC guidelines.

\section{Clinical Implications and Future Directions }
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Clinical Significance}

NutriBiteBot addresses a key gap in clinical nutrition: the automated
reconciliation of conflicting dietary guidelines for patients with
multiple comorbidities such as hypertension (HTN), type~2 diabetes
mellitus (T2DM), and chronic kidney disease (CKD). Current systems
typically apply population-level thresholds independently, requiring
manual reconciliation by dietitians when recommendations conflict.

Manual reconciliation of multiple guideline sets can require
30--60~minutes per consultation and is known to be
error-prone~\cite{kdoqi2020}. In India, where the
dietitian-to-population ratio is approximately
1:333{,}000~\cite{varma2023} and clinical dietitian-to-patient ratios
frequently exceed 1:100 in accredited hospitals and surpass 1:200 in
non-accredited settings~\cite{phfi2012}, this becomes a major
scalability constraint. NutriBiteBot addresses this bottleneck by
automating four steps: laboratory-driven risk stratification, nutrient
budget derivation, ingredient-level portion computation, and
guideline-conflict resolution.

The system introduces four key capabilities. First, conflicting
guidelines across HTN, T2DM, and CKD are resolved using a
deterministic KDIGO-compliant constraint hierarchy
(Section~\ref{sec:portion}). Second, portion limits are derived from
patient-specific laboratory values through the sigmoid
severity-to-fraction mapping (Eq.~\ref{eq:sigmoid}) rather than
population thresholds. Third, ingredient detection is linked to the
IFCT~2017 database~\cite{ifct2017}, enabling nutrient-aware portion
computation for Indian foods. Fourth, the bounded LLM architecture
restricts Groq Llama-3.3 to operate within the hard gram-level limits
computed by the rules engine, ensuring clinical safety.

\subsection{Future Research Directions}

Future work will focus on three main directions. First, prospective
clinical validation will be conducted by comparing model predictions
with dietitian-labelled sensitivity classifications in a nephrology
outpatient cohort to assess real-world clinical reliability. Second,
uncertainty-aware dietary recommendations will be explored by
propagating probabilistic model uncertainty through the
severity-to-fraction mapping, enabling the system to generate credible
intervals for gram-level portion recommendations. Finally, the system
will be expanded to support additional comorbidities, multilingual
patient-facing output, integration with ABDM-compliant electronic
health record infrastructure, and improved computer vision coverage
for packaged and processed foods.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented NutriBiteBot, an interpretable, locally-deployed clinical decision support system for ingredient-level dietary portion recommendation in patients with co-existing HTN, T2DM, and CKD. The system achieves near-perfect multi-target sensitivity classification (mean accuracy~0.9991, mean $\kappa$~0.9983 on the TabNet clinical model), resolves inter-condition dietary conflicts via a deterministic hierarchical rules engine, computes ingredient-specific safe gram quantities via a novel sigmoid severity-to-fraction formulation grounded in IFCT~2017, and generates bounded, clinically safe recipe recommendations via a generative agent. A systematic evaluation of TabNet demonstrates that TabNet's native per-instance feature attribution, sparse attention-based feature selection, and structural compatibility with monotonic preprocessing constraints make it the preferred architecture for clinical tabular inference in this setting, despite near-identical aggregate classification accuracy.
All components execute within a local Docker environment, satisfying DPDPA~2023 patient data sovereignty requirements and making NutriBiteBot directly deployable in Indian hospital and clinic settings. The containerised architecture supports horizontal scaling across multiple concurrent patient sessions without architectural modification, as each inference request is stateless at the model layer and session state is isolated within the per-patient nutrient ledger persisted to the local Supabase instance. TabNet inference on the 14-feature clinical vector completes within milliseconds on CPU, and the portion computation engine operates at negligible latency relative to the upstream vision detection step, making the end-to-end pipeline suitable for real-time clinical use at the point of consultation. The Docker deployment model further supports installation across heterogeneous infrastructure, including hospital on-premises servers, clinic workstations, and telemedicine platforms operating within secured local networks, without requiring cloud provisioning or internet connectivity. This is particularly relevant for deployment in semi-urban and rural Indian healthcare facilities where network reliability cannot be assumed.
While the system is validated and immediately deployable within the Indian clinical context, being grounded in IFCT~2017 and compliant with Indian data protection legislation, the underlying architecture is database-agnostic. Substituting an alternate national food composition database and the applicable regional data governance framework is sufficient to extend deployment to international settings, without modification to the core classification, portion computation, or constraint resolution components. At scale, the modular pipeline architecture permits independent versioning and retraining of individual TabNet classifiers as updated clinical guidelines are published, for instance when incorporating future KDIGO or ADA revisions, without requiring full system redeployment, further supporting the long-term clinical maintainability of the framework.
